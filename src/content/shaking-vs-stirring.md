---
title: Shaking vs. Stirring
image: stirring.png
---

Shaking and stirring both have the same two objectives: to chill a drink and to dilute a drink. They just have very different ways of doing it. A shaken drink (such as a Daiquiri) should be "alive"; spritely, aerated, complete with a thin head of miniscule ice chips and air bubbles, teaming and fizzing impressively. In addition to the two previous attributes, shaking a drink also aerates a drink. Drinks containing citrus should taste a little aerated and spritely and light on the tongue.

"Clear" drinks, should be stirred. A "Clear" drink is any drink that contains predominantly alcohol. This includes anything in the Manhattan, Old Fashioned, Martini or Negroni family. Drinks in these categories are definitely considered "sipping" drinks and are generally consumed at a slower rate than the more spritely, shaken drinks. There is one other key outcome to stirring a drink as opposed to shaking a drink and that is its end appearance. Stirring a drink does not result in a fine head of ice chips and bubbles, it results in no head whatsoever. This is ideal as it further enforces the drink as a heavy, silky smooth sipper, with no hint of aeration on the tongue.

The rule to come from this is as follows: Any drink containing any sort of modifier that isn't clear (i.e. juice, cream, eggs) should be shaken hard to appropriately combine all the ingredients and give the drink a slightly aerated finish. Any drink that is all or predominantly alcohol, you should stir. This is to maintain its heavy look and silky-smooth feel in the mouth.
